Introduction

In late 2020, de Blasio unveiled the largest one-year bike and bus lane expansion in NYC history. Since then, he has created miles of bike lanes, and the Department of Transportation has recently released its 5-year plan in which they hope to achieve 250 miles of protected bike lanes by 2026. Bike ridership has been a hot topic in the city, and bike share systems like CitiBike have been expanding rapidly over the past few years. Citibike is a privately owned public bicylcle sharing system that allows users to rent a bike that is parked at multiple stations throughout the city, and drop it off at another station when finished. I want to analyze the bike trends in NYC to see if this policy has made an impact on bike ridership, based on the change in bike riders using bike count data and bike-share (citiBike) usage. My objective is to find out whether or not we should continue pushing for bike expansion policies in the city, or if bike ridership has already reached its potential as a mode of transportation in NYC.

Overview

Although the push to increase bikers in the efforts of creating a green-er NYC, I was skeptical of the results of the bike lane expansion. Personally, I have noticed an increase in vehicular traffic and MTA ridership rather than see an increase in bikers. My hypothesis was that bike ridership is not a popular form of transportation in the city, and that ridership has been stagnant since the bike lane expansions. To discover whether or not my theory was correct, I used publicly available data to find the counts of bike riders over the last few years. I used Python 3.8, Pandas, and Plotly.

Data

  • Citibike Data

    Citibike publicly provides over a hundred csv files since 2013 up until October 2021. Each file contains each citiBike transaction and its associated trip duration, start time, end time, pick up location, drop off location, birth year, gender, and membership type.

  • NYC OpenData

    NYC Open Data releases bicycle counts conducted around NYC at key locations. Each line contains the bike counts in 15 minute increments, as well as the location. Bicycle counting technology is used, and the data is limited to select locations. Thus, this data has more limitations than the Citibike data .

Overall Conclusion and Analysis

My hypothesis was incorrect; the bike lane expansion policy as well as citiBike’s increasing presence in NYC have undeniably increased the amount of bikers in NYC over the years. There is a great upwards trends over the years, showing that biking is in fact not out of style, and will likely continue to prosper. However, we see that on average, the winter months see much less bicycle utilization. I propose that it may be more efficient would be to have more bike lanes in the summer and spring months, and reduce the number of lanes in the fall and spring. This would be much more efficient and cause less traffic in NYC.
